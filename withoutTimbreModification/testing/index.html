Cognition and Decision Making

You are invited to participate in a research study that examines how what we have learned can influence how people think, act, and make decisions. You were selected as a possible subject because you are a member of the Psychology Subject Pool. We ask that you read this form and ask any questions you may have before agreeing to be in the study.

The study is being conducted by Dr. Robert Goldstone in the Department of Psychological and Brain Sciences.

STUDY PURPOSE

The purpose of this study is to better understand the way that people see objects can change as they learn new tasks.

NUMBER OF PEOPLE TAKING PART IN THE STUDY

If you agree to participate, you will be one of approximately 5000 subjects who will be participating in this research.

PROCEDURES FOR THE STUDY

If you agree to be in the study, you will be seated at a computer monitor. You will be presented with several straightforward tasks to complete. These tasks will include responding with key strokes to stimuli (e.g., images, videos, words, sounds) displayed on a computer monitor and may involve learning what categories objects belong in, determining if objects are identical or related, and remembering or transferring what you learn about objects in one training task to a related task. The experiment will take up to 120 minutes to complete.

RISKS OF TAKING PART IN THE STUDY

Participation in this study involves a potential risk of loss of confidentiality. This risk is minimized by the investigator, as outlined in the “Confidentiality” section.

BENEFITS OF TAKING PART IN THE STUDY

An understanding of how people change the way they see the world as they learn can help us to find more efficient methods to teach information and develop technologies that increase the speed of learning. You benefit from this experience because you learn something about how an experiment is designed and conducted, what issues are of interest to cognitive scientists, and what kinds of group behaviors emerge when individuals try to reach their goals in an environment that consists largely of other individuals.

More about this experiment:

The goal of this experiment is to investigate a phenomenon known as auditory streaming, which is the process by which we separate the sounds in our environment into distinct sources. For example, imagine you are walking down a street; you may hear a car passing by, a dog barking, and a lawn mower running. Even though these sounds are hitting your eardrums all at once, you likely do not perceive them as one wall of noise; instead, you are able to parse each sound as belonging to a particular source using various cues (e.g. pitch, duration, timbre) and thus make sense of the world around you. Each of these perceived sources are referred to as auditory streams.

This same process occurs when you listen to a piece of music: you may be able to hear multiple different instruments at once, or you may hear the notes being played on one instrument as belonging to different melodies. For instance, if a pianist plays a walking bassline with their left hand and a lead melody with their right, you are likely to hear two distinct melodic lines at once; you have split the sound of the piano into two auditory streams. It is this type of auditory streaming that you likely experienced while responding to these trials: the grouping of notes within a melodic phrase into distinct melodic elements.

In particular, this study aims to investigate the cues people use in the process of grouping notes, particularly when timbre is identical between the different notes. The melodic patterns you listened to during this experiment were designed to be perceptually ambiguous to some degree by pitting certain cues against one another. For example, one interpretation of a pattern could suggest that differences in pitch are more salient than temporal differences for auditory streaming, while another interpretation could suggest the opposite.

Based on your responses and the responses of everyone else who has participated in this experiment, we hope to reach some conclusions about which cues are most salient in the process of auditory streaming.
